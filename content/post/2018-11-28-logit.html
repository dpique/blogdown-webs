--- title: What is the logit? author: Daniel Pique date: '2017-11-28' slug: logit categories: - math - probability tags: [] ---

The logit is defined as \(logit(p) = log(p/(1-p))\) and is also called the log odds. The “odds” are a ratio between 2 events – the probability of the event happening (p) and not happening (1-p). We see this as p/(1-p). The odds are wrapped in a logarithm – hence the log-odds.

The logit is used to transform probabilities from the open interval \((0,1)\) to the real number line (\(-\infty, \infty\)). Note that probabilities that equal exactly 0 or 1 would lead to a logit that is undefined.

The distribution of many of these mapped probability values is often assumed to have a Normal (Gaussian) distribution. This can help in Bayesian sampling frameworks to make it more convenient to sample from the posterior distribution.

Note that the inverse logit takes you from logit values (infinite domain) back to probabilities. The inverse logit is also known as the logistic function: \(logistic(v) = e^v /{1+e^v}\).

Mnemonic – logit has infinite domain; logistic has tiny domain (0,1)
